1. Analysis Questions¶

The following questions are t to be explored in this data analysis:

  • Which symptoms are most strongly associated with diabetes, and why might these relationships exist?
  • How do different symptoms interact to influence diabetes risk?
  • How does age influence the presence of diabetes, and does it change the importance of other symptoms?
  • Are there distinct groups of patients based on symptoms, and do these groups differ in diabetes outcomes?
  • Why do some individuals without key symptoms still get classified as diabetic?
  • Can a small subset of symptoms explains most diabetes cases, and what does this suggest about early detection?

2. Dataset Description¶

The dataset used in this study consists of 520 patient records, each observed on a set of physiological factors that is maybe associated with diabetes. The outcome variable, class, indicates whether a patient is categorized as Positive (early diabetic) or Negative (non-diabetic).

The dataset contains one continuous variable (Age) and 15 categorical predictor variables, each describing the presence or absence of a clinical condition or symptom. These variables include polyuria, polydipsia, sudden weight loss, weakness, polyphagia, partial paresis, and others that are known early clinical indicators of diabetes. Categorical variables are encoded using binary values (“Yes”/“No” or “Male”/“Female”).

The dataset was also collected from Syllhet Diabetes Hospital, Syllhet, Bangladesh

3. Data Wrangling¶

Since there were no missing values in the dataset, there was no data cleaning conducted. However, since the categorical variables were in string format, the values were encoded into 0s and 1s. Specifically, 1 for 'Female', 'Positive', or 'Yes'; and 0 for 'Male', 'Negative', or 'No'.

4. Exploratory Data Analysis¶

We can visually see from the distribution of the target classes the distribution is slightly imbalanced. It is supported by the percentage displayed above with 61.5% of the class consists of the positive class(early-diabetic) and 38.5% is of the negative class. However, since it hovers around the 60-40 split, this is approximately a fairly balanced dataset.

According to the age boxplot, early diabetic correspondents have a median age of 48 and a mean of 49.07 which indicates a right skewed distribution. Furthermore, the age range of early diabetics are 16-90. We also have observed some outliers specifically in early diabetic correspondents. Furthermore,we can also see that correspondents diagnosed with early diabetes tend to experience polyphagia, polyuria, polydipsia, partial paresis, and weakness. Particularly, the prevalence of the polyuria, polydipsia, and weakness was noticeably elevated.
